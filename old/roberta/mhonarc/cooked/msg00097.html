Apartment hunting has taught me a few things about my neighbourhood. 
For one, there are a lot of really, really nice people around.  For
another, there are a lot of really, really crappy places to live.  

Over the last couple of weeks I have walked up and down every street
within a half hour radius of my office ? that?s a lot of streets.  Not
just once mind you, but repeatedly.  Unlike the apartment hunting that I
am used to in the ?west?, through newspaper or on-line ads, housing
offices or friends, here the most ?efficient? way to find something is
to go from phone pole to phone pole and read the handwritten fliers, or
simply to look on the front gates of the houses going by.  The papers,
if you can read the runny ink after the spring rains, say how many rooms
are available for rent, occasionally how much money they are expecting
to get, and a phone number or two.  There?s never an address listed,
making it pretty much essential that you keep your phone handy.

People who answer the phone generally fall into 2 categories?those who
are patient with my halting language skills and those who respond to my
fumbling with rapid-fire tirades followed abruptly by the dial tone. 
Luckily, there have been more of the former than the latter (which has
pleasantly surprised me, given that my experience on the street so far
has mostly been the opposite).  My Korean is getting the workout of the
century, first trying to figure out where the actual rooms are located
on these convoluted streets (where not just one but TWO different sets
of addresses are in active use), and then finding out the details of the
space and whether or not I can afford it.

Unfortunately, patience and kindness on the phone are no indication of
the quality of the rooms available?which are mostly awful.  I have been
in Korea long enough not to expect much in the way of size, but even
still I am shocked by just how many truly miniscule apartments there are
for rent.  And by how rampant mold problems are around here.  One
charming spot I saw this morning was so mold-ridden that when I opened
the door I felt like a whole army of tiny green spores were hacking at
my nose with musty little axes.  The walls were the same colour as the
mossy stones at the temple up the road.  ?Don?t worry,? said the helpful
adjumma in Korean, ?we?re going to re-wallpaper before you move in.?

The few spots that aren?t totally depressing are exorbitantly priced. 
The traditional rental system in Korea relies on ?key money? rather than
on monthly rent.  You pay a big bundle upfront, which the landlords
invest while you live in the space rent-free (or, more recently, with a
minimal monthly fee), and when you move out they refund the total
original amount.  Key money for a decent 2 room apartment around my
house (smaller than the smallish one bedroom apartment I had in Edmonton
while I was going to university, for those of your who remember it) runs
between 40 and 60 million won ? about 40 ? 60 thousand dollars.  Yikes! 
A little out of my budget.  When I tell people that I have about a
thousand dollars available for key money they?re so flabbergasted they
don?t even laugh (a typical response to embarrassment here).  One old
man summed up my problem thus: ?Come back when you have money.?

On the very rare occasion that I have found something that is reasonably
sized, reasonably clean, AND where the landlord is willing to negotiate
price into my ballpark (a mean feat indeed!), the apartments are snapped
up at light speed.  Twice the places that I was looking at were rented
between the time I called to make the appointment to see the space and
when I walked the 2-5 block distance from the flier to the house. 
People don?t plan much in advance to move into the kind of places I am
in the market for, so the first one they see that?s halfway decent they
snap up immediately.  No time for mulling things over.  This means that
if you are decision-impaired like I am, and need time to think about
things, then you are probably SOL.

And yet, despite all these assorted traumas of looking (I can?t
emphasize enough how demoralizing it is to see hole after hole after
hole and suddenly realize how many people around me live in places like
that), I have managed to find a new apartment!  And a couple new friends
as well.  Yippee!!  And all of that just 4 short blocks from where I
live now.  A mere few hours ago I would not have thought it possible.

On my unhappy way home this morning after a marathon 4 hours of walking,
calling and looking, I happened to see a flier stuck to the wall of a
new house that hadn?t been there when I?d set out.  I figured I?d
already called a couple of dozen places, what was one more?  From the
moment I stepped inside I knew it was the one.  High ceilings, lots of
(unbarred!!) windows, a cute little private patio that?s about 2 feet
wide and 15 feet long.  Built in gas stove, washing machine (in the
kitchen, naturally), air conditioner.  Storage room.  All of it so new
that the plastic covers hadn?t even been removed from the window frames
and door knobs yet.  And landlords willing to accept my paltry one
million key money in exchange for a raised monthly rent.  Unbelievable.

Even though I was about 85% sold on it immediately, I still wanted to
talk it over with a couple of people and have my Korean friend Anna look
at it before I signed on the dotted line (and handed over my only wad of
cash ? I won?t get the key money back from the house I?m in now until
the start of July).  I made an appointment with the real-estate agent
handling the property for later in the afternoon and went on with my
day.  2 hours before our appointed time he called to make sure I was
still coming in?two other people had made better offers than mine and
were standing in his office.  Anna graciously agreed to meet me early
and much to the other renters? dismay, half an hour later the place was
officially mine.  I think they?re still in shock that the realtor
accepted less money just because I?d seen him first.  Thank God for
small miracles.  

So June 2nd is moving day!  Classes will be done for the semester
(halleluiah!), and I will have a 2 week grace-period after that before I
have to be totally out of my house.  A good chance to whittle down the
mass of stuff I have accumulated over the last 5 years here and do most
of the moving by hand (I?ll hire a truck at the end for the big stuff). 
Also a good chance to say good-bye to this much-loved home slowly,
rather than in a box-maddened rush at the last possible minute.  If I
have to leave, which of course I do, then this is as good a way to do it
as I could imagine.

As for the new friends, it turns out that one of the apartments I looked
at a few days ago (which I could definitely NOT afford) is owned by a
woman who goes to the same church as Anna.  They didn?t know each other,
but when she saw us together and then saw Anna at church this morning
she put two and two together and gave me a call.  Her house is half way
between where I live now and where I?m moving to ? just around the
corner from Anna?s own house.  It seems that all the church women had a
pow-wow after services discussing my ?renting problem? (an entertaining
and slightly horrifying thought) and came up with a plan to help me. 
When they learned I?d found a place they were delighted and instead of
enacting the plan had Anna and I over to the house for tea.  30 minutes
of light refreshments turned into several courses of food and a
sing-along lasting several hours.  I have never seen Anna talk and laugh
so much in my life!  The whole thing was a huge success and they?re all
getting together again later this week, with or without me.  

AND my garden cat had kittens this morning.  What a great day!

Be well, much love to you all!  R
